About

I am an independent researcher exploring social movements, political transformations, and the neuroscience of collective behavior. My work focuses on how societies and communities move from victimhood to strategic agency and long-term self-determination.

Research Interests

  • Revolutions and regime change dynamics
  • Social psychology and collective behavior
  • Neuroscience of decision-making under pressure
  • Historical and comparative studies of political transitions

Current Work

I am currently engaged in a broad interdisciplinary study examining how nations and communities move beyond a victim identity to take collective responsibility for their future. This inquiry draws inspiration from historical examples such as the long-term strategies developed by the Jewish people, who transformed centuries of persecution into resilience, planning, and self-determination.

My focus is on understanding:

  • What internal and external factors enable a society to shift from seeing itself primarily as a victim to becoming an active agent of change?
  • How do education, cultural narratives, and leadership contribute to decade-long planning and sustained organizational capacity?
  • To what extent can models of resilience and collective responsibility be adapted for nations with different histories, values, and social dynamics?

To explore these questions, I am reviewing literature in political psychology, social neuroscience, and historical case studies of nation-building. I am also analyzing how identity formation, intergenerational memory, and cultural institutions shape a people's capacity to organize strategically and pursue long-term goals.
